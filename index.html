What the data actually says

Not rules — patterns. Tap any card to learn more.

🧍

Designate a sober person before you go out

Strongest effect
Groups with at least one sober member show significantly fewer incidents — not just in traffic, but in all conflict situations. The effect is strongest when the sober person is socially central to the group.
One sober person → up to 60% reduction in escalation risk
👁️

Watch for escalation signals early

Time-sensitive
Most incidents follow a predictable build-up window of 10–15 minutes. Raised voices, physical posturing, and repeated grievances are reliable early signals. Acting early matters more than acting right.
Intervening at signal stage is 3× more effective than at confrontation stage
🚶

The exit beats the argument

Counterintuitive
De-escalation works well at low intoxication. Above a certain threshold, verbal intervention tends to prolong or escalate incidents. Leaving the situation — even mid-conflict — is consistently the most effective outcome.
Leaving during escalation resolves 80%+ of incidents without harm
🕐

Pace matters as much as quantity

Often overlooked
Rapid intoxication — typical in pre-gaming or shots — compresses the vulnerability and aggression curves. The same number of drinks spread over 4 hours produces a significantly different risk profile than over 1 hour.
Pacing over 3+ hours halves peak aggression potential vs. rapid drinking
🤝

Most people want the same night you do

Reframe
Survey data consistently shows that the majority of people in nightlife settings want a good, conflict-free night. Incidents are driven by a small minority. Bystander awareness is one of the most scalable protective factors.
~85% of bystanders say they would intervene — but most wait for someone else to go first
