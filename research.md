# Research abstract: alcohol, violence & group dynamics in Sweden

Sources: CAN (Centralförbundet för alkohol- och narkotikaupplysning), BRÅ (Brottsförebyggande rådet), Norström & Pape (2010), Norström & Rossow (2012). For future integration into joyeu.se.

---

## Alcohol and violence

Alcohol is a triggering factor in violence, not the sole cause. Most situations involving alcohol do not lead to violence, and many violent incidents occur without alcohol — but the correlation is strong and well-documented.

**Key figures (Sweden):**

- 74% of men who experienced assault reported the perpetrator was under the influence of alcohol or drugs (BRÅ, NTU 2012)
- 53% of male assault victims were themselves intoxicated at the time
- For women: 51% of perpetrators were intoxicated, 20% of victims were — lower figures because violence against women more often occurs at home rather than in public settings
- Each extra hour of bar opening time produces a measurable increase in violent crime — documented across 18 Norwegian cities (Norström & Rossow, 2012)
- The effect of alcohol on violent behaviour is strongest among those already predisposed to aggression (Norström & Pape, 2010)
- 14% of Swedes report having been negatively affected by someone else's drinking in the past 12 months

## Alcohol's effect on behaviour by intoxication level

Based on blood alcohol concentration (BAC), the following effects are well-established:

| BAC | Typical effects |
|-----|----------------|
| 0.2‰ | Relaxation, reduced self-criticism |
| 0.5‰ | Inhibitions lowered, slower reflexes, impaired judgment, reduced ability to process information |
| 0.8‰ | Overconfidence, loud and imprecise movement, smell of alcohol |
| 1.0‰ | Slurred speech, impaired muscle control and emotional regulation |
| 1.5‰ | Emotional outbursts, loss of balance, nausea |
| 2.0‰ | Double vision, unable to walk straight |
| 3.0‰+ | Near unconsciousness |

Women reach higher BAC than men at the same intake due to lower body water content and lower levels of alcohol-metabolising enzymes. Drinking on an empty stomach or rapidly increases peak BAC significantly.

## Young people in Sweden

From CAN's national school survey 2025 (Rapport 239, conducted spring 2025):

- 67% of gymnasium year 2 students (approx. age 17–18) drank alcohol in the past 12 months
- 36% of year 9 students (approx. age 15–16) drank in the past 12 months — meaning **64% did not**
- Alcohol consumption among Swedish school students has declined significantly since the early 2000s and is **below the European average**
- The declining trend has levelled off in recent years

From Vanor och konsekvenser 2025 (Rapport 242, adults 18–84):

- 10% of adults 18–84 meet criteria for at least mild alcohol dependency (DSM-5)
- In the **18–29 age group there is no gender difference** in alcohol dependency — unlike all older age groups where men are more affected
- Alcohol dependency has decreased since 2017, but only significantly among men

## Implications for the model

These findings support the dual-axis model (vulnerability + aggression) used in joyeu.se:

1. Alcohol raises both the risk of being harmed (vulnerability) and the risk of causing harm (aggression), but the aggression effect is concentrated among those already predisposed to it
2. The 0.5–1.0‰ range is the critical window where judgment and emotional control deteriorate while the person still appears functional — this is when early detection matters most
3. The large share of non-drinking young people (64% in year 9, ~33% in gymnasium year 2) represents a natural pool for the sober anchor role — normalising non-drinking is supported by the data
4. The gender asymmetry in violence (men more often perpetrators and victims in public settings) is relevant context for how the site communicates risk
